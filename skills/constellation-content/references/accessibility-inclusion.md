# Accessibility & Inclusion

Source: [Zillow Style Guide — Accessibility & inclusion](https://zillow.styleguide.com/zg-style-guide-2-0/accessibility-inclusion)

## Why Inclusive Language Matters

Empathy is a cornerstone of Zillow's user experience work and a principle of our brand voice. We want our experiences to inspire confidence, build trust, and create possibility. This goal isn't possible if some users feel excluded.

It's particularly important for Zillow's product content to be inclusive because of historically inequitable and discriminatory practices in the real estate, mortgage, and rental sectors.

---

## Inclusive Language Rules

- Avoid gendered, ableist, or exclusionary language
- Use person-first language: "people with disabilities" over "disabled people" (Zillow standard)
- Ensure content is culturally inclusive and avoids unnecessary assumptions
- Singular "they" is acceptable for referring to an individual whose pronouns are unknown
- Do not assume pronouns based on how a name might traditionally read

### Assessing a Word's Impact

When deciding if a word may be discriminatory, ask:
1. Is the language being used metaphorically? What are the implications?
2. Does the language imply "otherness" and promote exclusivity?
3. Can the language be replaced with something more clear and literal?
4. Are there groups of people who could be harmed? Who and how?
5. Does the language make you uncomfortable, even if you can't explain why?

### Terms to Avoid

| Don't Use | Use Instead | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| blacklist | block, blocked users | Racism and oppression |
| whitelist | approved, allow list | Racism and oppression |
| white glove | (describe service directly) | Racism and oppression |
| master / master bedroom | primary / primary bedroom | Exclusionary |
| his-and-her | (non-gendered description) | Heteronormative |
| Jack-and-Jill | (non-gendered description) | Heteronormative, culturally specific |
| mother-in-law suite | (describe the space) | Ageist and sexist |
| walk-in closet | (use dimensions) | Ableist |
| walking distance | (use actual distance/time) | Ableist |
| walkthrough | tour, video tour | Ableist |
| handicap, handicapped | disability, person with a disability | Outdated, exclusionary |
| handi-capable, differently abled | person with a disability | Euphemisms — not preferred |
| disable/enable | turn off/on, deactivate/activate, allow/show | "Disable" implies negative state |

### Dual Heritage
- Don't hyphenate: African American, Asian American (not African-American)

### Gender & Identity
- LGBTQ+ — always include the plus (exception to AP)
- Use singular "they" when pronouns are unknown
- Don't use gendered terms unless specifically relevant

## Writing for Accessibility

### Make Content Perceivable

- Write with screen readers in mind — keep content clear and easy to read (and listen to)
- Include heading text so screen reader users can navigate sections
- Provide alt text for images
- Use emojis only when they enhance text, never in repetition (screen readers read each one aloud)
- Ensure the emoji's alt text matches your messaging
- Important alerts should remain on screen until the user dismisses them (accommodate slower reaction times)

### Make Content Understandable

- Present only the info users need, and only when they need it
- Keep sentences simple and short — generally no longer than 20 words
- Limit paragraphs to 3–5 sentences
- Use headings and subheadings to create structure and describe content
- Break complex tasks into smaller steps with clear instructions
- Spell out acronyms and abbreviations on first use

### Use Plain Language

- Avoid jargon, colloquial language, and slang
- Use familiar, everyday words that don't rely on metaphors or culturally specific experiences
- If technical or legal terms are necessary, explore providing a plain language glossary
- Address privacy concerns using simple terms to explain why personal information is collected

## Multimedia Accessibility

### Video

- **Give users control:** Avoid autoplaying content; allow users to control playback
- **Captions:** Should appear in real time and match spoken words. Two lines per frame is a good rule of thumb. Include non-speech sounds (e.g., [music playing]). Avoid auto-generated captions without review. Use sentence case
- **Transcripts:** Offer a separate text-based transcript with dialogue and important visual descriptions. Use clear speaker identification. Make the transcript easy to access
- **Audio descriptions:** Include descriptions of visual information needed to understand the video content. Can be integrated narration, alternative video, or separate synced file
